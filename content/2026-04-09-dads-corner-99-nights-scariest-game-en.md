## A dad, a kid, and a very dark forest

Hello everyone! FaionEnder here — also known as "dad who accidentally became a Roblox player." 🧔

So my daughter (you know her as EnderFaion) has been bugging me for WEEKS to play this game called **"99 Nights in the Forest"** with her. "Come on dad, it's not even that scary!" she said. "It'll be fun!" she said.

Reader, it was not "not even that scary."

## What is 99 Nights in the Forest?

For those who don't know, it's a Roblox horror survival game where you and your friends get stuck in a forest for — you guessed it — 99 in-game nights. Each night gets progressively more terrifying, with new creatures, challenges, and jump scares.

The concept is simple: survive until dawn, gather resources during the day, build defenses, and try not to scream at your computer at 2 AM while your spouse is sleeping.

...not that I would know anything about that last part.

## Night 1-10: "This isn't so bad"

The first few nights lulled me into a false sense of security. You walk around, collect sticks, build a little campfire. I was feeling pretty confident.

"See?" I told my daughter. "Dad's got this. No monster is going to scare me."

Famous last words.

## Night 11-30: "What was that noise?"

This is where the game starts messing with you. Strange sounds in the distance. Shadows moving between trees. Your flashlight starts flickering for no reason.

I'll admit — I may have yelped once. ONCE. My daughter, of course, recorded it and threatened to send it to the family group chat. Kids these days have zero mercy.

The shadow creatures that appear around night 15 are genuinely well-designed. They don't just jump out at you — they stalk you. You'll see them in the corner of your screen, and when you turn to look, they're gone. Classic horror, and it works brilliantly.

## Night 31-60: "I need a break... for reasons"

Okay so here's where I'll confess something. Around night 40, there's this thing called **The Watcher**. It's a tall, pale figure that just... stands there. In the trees. Watching you.

It doesn't attack. It doesn't move. It just WATCHES.

And somehow that's worse than any jump scare. My daughter was laughing at me because apparently I kept turning my character to check if it was still there. (It always was.)

The resource management gets real around this point too. You need to plan your supplies carefully, build better shelters, and work as a team. My daughter became the strategist. I became the guy who carried sticks and screamed.

A good team dynamic, honestly.

## Night 61-99: "WE'RE IN THIS TOGETHER"

The final stretch is absolutely wild. New creatures every few nights, your safe zones get smaller, and the forest literally changes around you. Paths you used before are gone. Trees move when you're not looking.

Night 77 has a particular event that I won't spoil, but I will say this: my daughter screamed. MY DAUGHTER. The one who said "it's not even that scary." HA! Sweet vindication.

We finally completed all 99 nights at around midnight on a Saturday. We high-fived. We celebrated. I may have shed a single dad tear of pride.

## Why it's actually great for families

Here's the thing — underneath all the scares, **99 Nights in the Forest** is actually a fantastic co-op experience. Here's why I recommend it for families:

1. **Teamwork:** You literally can't survive alone past night 30. You need to communicate, plan, and work together.
2. **Problem-solving:** The resource management and base building require actual strategy.
3. **Bonding:** Nothing brings a family closer together than screaming at the same thing simultaneously.
4. **Age-appropriate:** The scares are atmospheric, not gory. It's spooky, not traumatizing.
5. **Achievement:** Completing all 99 nights together is a genuine accomplishment.

## Dad's final rating

**8.5/10** — Would get scared with my kid again.

The game is incredibly well-made for a Roblox experience. The atmosphere, sound design, and progressive difficulty are all top-notch. My only complaint is that some of the mid-game nights (around 40-55) feel a bit repetitive before it picks back up.

But seriously — if you're a parent looking for something to play with your kids that's actually engaging for adults too, give this one a shot. Just... maybe don't play it right before bed.

And if your kid asks "are you scared, dad?" — the correct answer is always "no." Even when the answer is very much yes.

Until next time, gamers (and fellow dads). FaionEnder out! 🧔✌️
